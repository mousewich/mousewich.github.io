Exciting Chess

What is "Exciting Chess"?

The pithy answer is that Exciting Chess is chess but more exciting. The better answer is that Exciting Chess is an improvisation game based on the structure of chess. I drafted the first informal rules of Exciting Chess during my time in college studying game design. I became very interested in the study of emergent gameplay and found myself constantly surrounded by theater performers and comedians: a perfect pool of test subjects for my profane experiments (showing up to game night with a scrappy prototype and the desire to make something fun).

The rules of Exciting Chess

Exciting Chess is an improvisation game and the way each match plays out will vary significantly based on the players and any house rules in place. The core rules of play are based on the eponymous game of chess. If you are unfamiliar with the rules of chess, it would be wise to learn them before attempting to play Exciting Chess.

Exciting Chess has one primary rule that distinguishes it from chess: Any player may, at their own discretion, take any action which conflicts with the rules or draw inferences about the state of conflict at play. An example action may be suggesting that a Pawn flee (move backward) in the face of a substantial threat or that a King may stand its ground and choose to remain in check until captured. An example inference may be that the loss of both Bishops from a board has had an impact on the religion of the pieces in play or that a messy divorce between a player's King and Queen will have a negative impact on morale.

Any action or inference which is accepted by all players may be codified as a house rule for exciting chess. These actions become legal moves and these inferences become facts about the world of play. Actions may be influenced by inferences which may, in turn, require the occurrence of specific actions to become true.

My house rules for Exciting Chess

If you are interested in playing Exciting Chess and constructing your own library of house rules organically, you have already learned everything you need to know. If you wish to see the rule set I have polished over several years of board game nights and impromptu play sessions, read on.

Pieces

King

The King is the ruling monarch of each player's board. The king must be captured like any other piece to end the game. The king moves exactly one square horizontally, vertically, or diagonally. Modified castling rules apply.

Queen

The Queen is a monarch. The Queen moves any number of vacant squares horizontally, vertically, or diagonally.

Castle (Rook)

The Castle is a structure and a noble's home. The Castle moves any number of vacant squares horizontally or vertically. Modified castling rules apply.

Bishop

The Bishop is a representative of the church. The Bishop moves any number of vacant spaces diagonally.

Knight

The Knight moves to one of the nearest squares not on the same row, column, or diagonal. The knight may jump pieces. The knight is a mounted unit.

Pawn

The Pawn is an infantry unit. As in chess, the Pawn has more movement rules than the other pieces. The Pawn moves straight forward one square but may only do so if that space is vacant. If the Pawn has not yet moved, the Pawn may instead move two square forward as long as both square are vacant. The Pawn can not move backward. The Pawn can capture an enemy piece in either of the two squares ahead of it diagonally but cannot move to those squares when they are vacant. Modified en passant and promotion rules apply.
Promotion
A Pawn may be promoted when it moves to an opposing player's home provided that its starting position was within a different player's home or barracks.

Rules

Check and Checkmate

Unlike Chess, a King may choose to endanger itself and remain in Check. Any player who places an opposing King in Check may announce that they have done so, but the defending player may choose not to move the targeted King out of danger. There is no Checkmate in Exciting Chess. A king may be captured like any other piece.

En Passant

Promotion

A piece up for promotion may be replaced with any unit of a higher rank.

Determination of Victory

Peace

Players may, at any time, make a deal for peace. As long as the conditions of the deal remain fulfilled, these players will remain at peace. If all surviving players are at peace with one-another, these players jointly win the game. A player may choose to redeclare war at any point, though doing so may have consequences (such as the betrayed player being upset).

Elimination of the Chain of Command

A player loses when they are no longer able to effectively issue orders of war while remaining in conflict with another player. The most common way to reach this end condition is the capture of a player's King when a line of royal succession has not been established, though there may be other ways to trigger this end.

The Gay Agenda

If a majority of pieces controlled by all surviving players are gay married and the religious institutions in play are eliminated or supportive, the gays win. All conflicts end immediately. Peace deals which are in opposition to The Gay Agenda end. Civil unions do not count toward a marriage majority but they are a step in the right direction.

Points

Points are everything and good game design demands they be complicated. Several actions in Exciting Chess will generate points. Each rule around points will describe which win condition these points contribute toward and how points are earned toward that goal.
